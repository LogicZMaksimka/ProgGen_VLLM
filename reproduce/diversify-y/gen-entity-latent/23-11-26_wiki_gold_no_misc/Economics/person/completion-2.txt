1. Adam Smith - a Scottish economist and philosopher who is considered the father of modern economics and author of "The Wealth of Nations".
2. John Maynard Keynes - a British economist whose ideas had a major impact on modern economic and political theory and the development of the modern macroeconomics.
3. Milton Friedman - an American economist who received the 1976 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his research on consumption analysis, monetary history and theory, and the complexity of stabilization policy.
4. Amartya Sen - an Indian economist and philosopher who was awarded the 1998 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his work on welfare economics and the concept of "capability approach".
5. Esther Duflo - a French economist who, along with Abhijit Banerjee and Michael Kremer, was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 2019 for their experimental approach to alleviating global poverty.
6. Paul Samuelson - an American economist who was the first American to win the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, and the author of "Economics: An Introductory Analysis," one of the most influential textbooks in the field.
7. Ludwig von Mises - an Austrian economist known for his work on praxeology and the Austrian School of economics, particularly his advocacy for laissez-faire capitalism.
8. Thomas Piketty - a French economist who gained international recognition for his book "Capital in the Twenty-First Century," which focuses on wealth and income inequality.
